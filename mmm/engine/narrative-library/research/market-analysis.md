# Market Analysis & Personalization/Retention Research — “Mmm!” (Morning Motion Multivitamin)

## TL;DR

- **The personalized-vitamin category is real and growing but unforgiving**: the US personalized-nutrition segment is ~$4.6B growing ~16.3% CAGR (Vision Research Reports), inside a global supplements market Grand View Research valued at $209.5B in 2025. Yet Care/of — despite Bayer’s ~$225M acquisition — canceled all subscriptions and laid off its 143 staff in June 2024. Winners retain by turning a product into a **daily ritual and a relationship**; losers over-index on discount-driven acquisition and quiz-only “personalization theater.”
- **Conversion and retention are won on two levers**: (1) “made for you” framing (McKinsey: personalization most often drives a 10–15% revenue lift, 5–25% range; product quizzes convert 5x+ site baseline) and (2) **habit formation** (Lally 2010: median ~66 days to automaticity, and missing one day doesn’t break the process). Supplement subscription churn runs ~5–8%/month (best-run 4–7%; 8–12% category average), with **12–20% churning in month 1** — so onboarding, expectation-setting, and “never run out” mechanics matter more than any single ad.
- **Everything must stay in DSHEA/FTC structure/function language** (“supports focus,” “helps maintain calm”) and avoid disease claims (“treats anxiety,” “reduces inflammation,” “relieves joint pain”). This report flags the exact lines that cross into prohibited territory per FDA 21 CFR 101.93 and FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance, and gives compliant rewrites per segment.

# PART 1 — MARKET ANALYSIS

## 1. Market Landscape & Trends (2024–2026)

**Category size and growth.** The global dietary supplements market was valued at $209.5B in 2025 and is projected to reach $431.7B by 2033 (9.5% CAGR) per Grand View Research. Grand View’s U.S. Dietary Supplements Market Report states: “The U.S. dietary supplements market size was estimated at USD 68.74 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach USD 131.08 billion by 2033, growing at a CAGR of 8.5% from 2026 to 2033.” Vitamins are the single largest ingredient segment (28.1% of 2025 US revenue); OTC products are 75.7% of the market and offline channels still 77.1%.

Baseline demand is deep and mainstream. Per the Council for Responsible Nutrition (CRN) 2023 Consumer Survey — conducted by Ipsos online Aug. 9–14, 2023 among 3,192 US adults 18+ — 74% of US adults take dietary supplements and 55% qualify as “regular users” (down from a 2021 high of 80%/61%), with 92% of users agreeing supplements are “essential to maintaining their health.” Multivitamins remain the most-used type (~70% of users). **Personalized nutrition specifically** was valued at $4.6B (US) growing at 16.3% CAGR toward ~$21B by 2033 (Vision Research Reports) — meaningfully faster than the base category, which is why the space keeps attracting entrants.

**The personalization shakeout — the Care/of cautionary tale.** This is the single most instructive story for “Mmm!” Care/of was founded in 2016 by Craig Elbert and Akash Shah, raised $46M, and had Bayer buy a 70% stake in 2020 in a deal reportedly valued at $225M. It then wound down in June 2024: per TechCrunch (June 15, 2024) and NutraIngredients-USA (June 7, 2024), Care/of canceled all subscriptions effective June 17, 2024, and a New York Department of Labor filing disclosed it “planned to lay off all 143 employees by July 3 due to a ‘funding loss.’” Bayer’s Christin Miller said “ceasing further investment in Care/of will allow Bayer to better invest in future innovations.” Post-mortems (NutraIngredients-USA) point to: (a) undifferentiated retail expansion (Target, Sam’s Club) where the personalization story didn’t translate on-shelf — one consultant said “I didn’t see its point of difference on the shelf”; (b) quiz-only personalization that critics called scientifically thin (Ritual: matching preferences like “more energy” is very different from identifying nutrient deficiencies); (c) post-COVID demand normalization; and (d) a parent company (Bayer) under Monsanto/Roundup litigation strain reallocating capital. **Lesson for “Mmm!”: quiz-based personalization is now “table stakes” (Suggestic), not a moat. The moat is retention, ritual, and relationship.**

**Competitive positioning map:**

- **Ritual** — anti-personalization; radical transparency, “clean,” one simple multivitamin, ~$30/mo, ~70% gross margins, ~$268M post-Series-B valuation (2022).  Voice is friendly, self-empowerment, “morning rituals,” “Make Your Self.” Now omnichannel (Whole Foods 2022, Target 2023). Uses an Apple Watch “habit loop” app to build repetition.
- **Persona (Nestlé Health Science)** — 100+ SKUs, comprehensive quiz including medications, nutrient-drug interaction screening, launched a white-label service in 2024 and Persona Pro (400+ practitioners in 2025). ~$40–80/mo.
- **Rootine** — nutrigenomics: DNA (~52 genes) + blood biomarkers + lifestyle, slow-release microbeads, ~$69–99/mo. Positions on precision/data; accepts external lab work.
- **Bioniq** — premium, blood-biomarker-driven AI algorithm (4M+ data points), sold in 60–70+ countries, $15M Series B July 2024 at a $75M valuation, Cristiano Ronaldo investor; entered an agreement to be acquired by Herbalife. Includes free Omega-3 in every batch.
- **HUM, Cuure, Everly, Vitally, VitaminLab, Sundose** — quiz-based, $30–60/mo band (the direct peer set for “Mmm!”).
- **Amazon / Subscribe & Save** — the elephant: ~77% of online vitamin sales;  structurally lower churn (3–6%/mo) because it rides an existing reorder pattern. This is “Mmm!”’s true price/convenience competitor, and the trigger for price-comparison cancellations.

**Where “Mmm!” sits:** Quiz-based, 4 SKUs, goal-segment personalization — essentially a leaner Care/of model. The differentiation must NOT be “we personalize” (commoditized) — it must be **RESPECT + RETURN + RITUAL** executed in copy and lifecycle.

**Trends shaping 2024–2026:**

- **Clean label / transparency is non-negotiable.** Acosta’s 2024 Clean Label Insights Study found 81% of shoppers consider it important to buy clean-label products. Mintel (Vitamins, Minerals & Supplements – US – 2025) found ingredient quality is cited by 40% of US supplement users as a key purchase factor and 27% are influenced by natural/organic ingredients; there is rising skepticism of “proprietary blends.” FDA recalls of tainted supplements (e.g., a December 2025 recall of a supplement spiked with an ED drug ingredient) are eroding baseline label trust — third-party verification (USP, NSF) is becoming a trust substitute.
- **Supplement stacking is mainstream.** Consumers assemble multi-product regimens, and “protocols beat single products” for retention (Joy Subscriptions): a single-SKU subscription is a commodity with near-zero switching cost, while a multi-product “protocol” becomes an identity — “A single-product subscription says ‘I buy this.’ A protocol subscription says ‘I follow this.’”
- **AI personalization** — reported at 92% business adoption; the category is migrating from quiz-only toward biomarkers.
- **Goal-based demand.** Mintel: consumers believe supplements help with immunity (41%), digestion/gut (29%), beauty (28%), energy (27%), and sleep (25%). Sleep is flagged as an under-served opportunity — ~40% of US consumers are concerned about sleep with relatively limited innovation.
- **Format innovation & pill fatigue** — gummies, strips, effervescents rising specifically to address “pill fatigue,” especially for people taking multiple supplements.
- **The MAHA movement** is amplifying natural-ingredient scrutiny, prompting reformulation reviews.

## 2. Buyer Motivations, Objections & Voice-of-Customer — Per Goal Segment

### ENERGY (base multivitamin MMM; B-vitamins/iron context)

- **Motivations:** Combat daily fatigue, “keep up” with demanding lives (work + parenting), fill dietary gaps, feel like the “best version of me.” Millennials are ~35% of supplement buyers; energy & weight management is the largest application segment (29.4% globally).
- **Emotional drivers:** Fear of underperforming, guilt about diet, desire for agency — post-pandemic, 58% say the desire to “take charge of my health” has increased (Champion Bio synthesis).
- **Objections:** “Do I really need this if I eat okay?”; efficacy skepticism (“vitamins just make expensive urine”); cost; “will I even feel anything?”
- **VOC language:** “tired all the time,” “no energy,” “afternoon slump / 2pm crash,” “drained,” “running on empty,” “need a pick-me-up that isn’t more coffee,” “sluggish.”

### STRESS (Ashwagandha AAH)

- **Motivations:** Manage a “high-alert,” always-on life; adaptogen interest is a durable trend, though growth has normalized post-COVID — SPINS natural-channel data (via Nutritional Outlook) show US ashwagandha sales grew 21% in 2021 but only 2% in 2022, with SPINS analyst Scott Dicker cautioning he “would not necessarily bet that it’s going to have these 30%, 40%, 50% growth years again.” (Treat any “+50% YoY / $140M” figures circulating in aggregators as unverified.) Buyers skew younger, urban, higher-income, and wellness-literate.
- **Emotional drivers:** Desire to feel calm/in-control, “turn my mind off,” build resilience.
- **Objections:** “Does adaptogen stuff actually work or is it woo?”; autoimmune/medication caution; skepticism of trendy botanicals.
- **VOC language:** “wired but tired,” “can’t switch off,” “on edge,” “frazzled,” “overwhelmed,” “need to take the edge off,” “mind won’t stop racing,” “burnt out.”

### SLEEP (Ashwagandha, Magnesium context; Omega-3 secondary)

- **Motivations:** Poor sleep quality even with adequate hours; a wind-down ritual. ~40% of US consumers are concerned about sleep (Mintel) — an under-served, high-opportunity segment.
- **Emotional drivers:** Craving restoration; dread of “another exhausted day.”
- **Objections:** Fear of dependency (vs. melatonin/Rx); “will it leave me groggy?”
- **VOC language:** “can’t wind down,” “toss and turn,” “wake up exhausted,” “tired but can’t sleep,” “racing mind at night,” “not rested.”

### FOCUS (Omega-3 OOO; multivitamin support)

- **Motivations:** Cognitive sharpness, combat “brain fog,” productivity, healthy mental aging. Omega-3 (DHA/EPA) is among the most-used specialty supplements (CRN); UK/EU data show cognitive function is a top-tier driver.
- **Emotional drivers:** Fear of mental decline; desire to feel sharp and clear.
- **Objections:** Fish-oil “fishy burps”/aftertaste; “does it actually reach the brain?”; vegan sourcing questions (algae).
- **VOC language:** “brain fog,” “can’t concentrate,” “foggy,” “scattered,” “can’t find the words,” “mentally drained,” “spacey,” “sharpness.”

### JOINTS / INFLAMMATION (Turmeric TASTY; Omega-3 secondary)

- **Motivations:** Stay active/mobile despite age, activity, or wear-and-tear; “keep doing what I love.” Turmeric/curcumin has been a strong grower. Buyers skew older (Boomers ~42% of US sales volume)  plus active/fitness adults.
- **Emotional drivers:** Refusal to “slow down”; desire to move without thinking about it.
- **Objections:** Curcumin bioavailability skepticism (“turmeric did nothing for me” — needs black pepper/piperine); underdosing concerns.
- **VOC language:** “creaky knees,” “stiff in the morning,” “achy,” “not as bendy as I used to be,” “everything cracks,” “stay active,” “keep moving,” “warm-up takes longer.”

## 3. DSHEA/FTC Compliance — Claims Guardrails (What to Avoid)

Per FDA 21 CFR 101.93 and the FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance (2022): supplements may make **structure/function** claims (how a nutrient supports normal body structure/function) but NOT **disease** claims (diagnose, treat, cure, mitigate, or prevent disease). All structure/function claims require the disclaimer: *“This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.”*  An HHS OIG review found 20% of sampled supplements carried prohibited disease claims   — a common, costly trap.

**PROHIBITED (disease claims) → SAFE (structure/function) rewrites:**

|Segment            |❌ Prohibited (do NOT use)                                                   |✅ Compliant                                                                                                    |
|-------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Stress             |“treats anxiety,” “relieves stress/depression”                              |“helps maintain a sense of calm,” “supports the body’s response to everyday stress”                            |
|Sleep              |“cures insomnia,” “treats sleep disorders”                                  |“supports restful sleep,” “helps you wind down”                                                                |
|Focus              |“prevents dementia/Alzheimer’s,” “treats ADHD”                              |“supports focus and mental sharpness,” “supports cognitive function”                                           |
|Joints/Inflammation|“reduces inflammation,” “relieves arthritis/joint pain,” “anti-inflammatory”|“supports joint comfort and mobility,” “supports a healthy inflammatory response,” “helps maintain flexibility”|
|Energy             |“treats chronic fatigue,” “cures anemia”                                    |“supports natural energy,” “helps fill nutritional gaps”                                                       |

**Key nuances flagged for the copy pipeline:**

1. FTC treats “helps” and “may” as still-substantiable claims — hedging words do NOT neutralize an implied disease claim. Per the FTC, “it’s not enough to say that the product ‘may’ have the claimed benefit or ‘helps’ achieve the claimed benefit.”
1. **Context creates implied claims:** imagery (e.g., someone clutching an aching joint, or people sneezing) plus a claim can create an implied disease claim even when the words alone are compliant (FTC “Cold Away” example).
1. “Supports a healthy inflammatory response” is the industry-standard compliant framing; “reduces inflammation” or “anti-inflammatory” as a benefit is a disease claim.
1. Never imply the product is a substitute for, or augments, a drug therapy, and never use disease names (e.g., “arthritis,” “insomnia,” “anxiety”) as targets.
